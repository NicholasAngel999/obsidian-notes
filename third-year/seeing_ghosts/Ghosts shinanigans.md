## ðŸ“Š Summary of the Census of Hallucinations Report

This study, the **Report on the Census of Hallucinations**, details the methodology and findings of a statistical inquiry into **spontaneous hallucinations**.

---

### 1. Scope of the Study: Types of Hallucination

The study limited its investigation to sensory hallucinations occurring in persons who believed themselves to be **completely awake** and **not suffering from delirium or insanity** or any other morbid condition. Specifically, they focused on hallucinations involving:

- **Sight** (seeing a living being or inanimate object).
    
- **Hearing** (hearing a voice).
    
- **Touch** (being touched by a living being or inanimate object).
    

The researchers avoided studying non-vocal sounds, such as footsteps, or hallucinations of taste, smell, and the muscular sense, due to the **high uncertainty of their phantasmal nature**, which would compromise statistical value.

---

### 2. Methodology: Selection of Participants and Information

The inquiry used a question known as the **"Census question"**:

> "HAVE you ever, when believing yourself to be completely awake, had a vivid impression of seeing, or being touched by a living being or inanimate object, or of hearing a voice; which impression, so far as you could discover, was not due to any external physical cause?".

- **Participants:** The question was asked of persons **over 21 years of age**. Collectors were instructed to ask the question **impartially and unselectively** of all sorts of people, as both "Yes" and "No" answers were equally important.
    
- **Information Collected:** Answers were recorded on **Schedule A**, along with the **name, address, sex, and occupation** of the person answering. Affirmative answers triggered a second form, **Schedule B**, requesting **details** about the experience, including the date, hour, occupation at the time, health, anxiety/grief, and whether others were present.
    

---

### 3. The Collectors

The data was gathered by **410 volunteer collectors**, consisting of **223 women** and **187 men**.

- **Affiliation:** About a **third** of the collectors were **members of the S.P.R.**, and another **sixth** were friends of S.P.R. members.
    
- **Status:** The **great majority** (about **nine-tenths**) of the collectors were **educated people**. Known occupations of collectors included: clerical (20), medical (22), and university or educational work (82).
    
- **Remuneration:** The work of all collectors was performed **entirely gratuitously**.
    

---

### 4. Criteria for Ruling a Story Out

The total of 2,272 affirmative answers required deductions because some did not fit the precise scope of the inquiry (353 cases were instantly judged as clearly outside the scope).

|Category of Exclusion|Specific Examples|
|---|---|
|**Morbid/Abnormal Physical State**|Hallucinations during illnesses where **delirium** is known to occur (e.g., scarlet or typhoid fever), or after temporary injury to the brain.|
|**Sleep/Transition Phenomena**|**Dream-images/nightmares** persisting into the waking state. **Voices or touches** rousing the percipient but **not continuing** when they woke. **Visions seen with closed eyes**.|
|**Lack of Specificity/Ambiguity**|**Sounds as of the human voice other than speech** (e.g., laughing, screaming, whispering where words weren't distinct). **Indefinite tactile impressions** (e.g., feelings of pressure through bedclothes).|
|**Visual Ambiguity**|Apparent **illumination of the place** where the percipient was. Objects seen **sideways out of the corner of the eye** (special probability of illusion). Lights seen **out of doors** (difficult to exclude real lights).|
|**Insufficient Account**|Experiences where the hallucination was deemed **not truly externalised** (internal mental image) or was a case of **mere illusion** or **mistaken identity**.|
