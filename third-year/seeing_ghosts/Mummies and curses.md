This text examines how rumours fueled the pervasive idea of the mummy's curse, particularly following the discovery of Tutankhamun's tomb.

Tutankhamun's Tomb and the Curse (1923):

The death of Lord Carnarvon in April 1923 was swiftly attributed to a curse by novelist Marie Corelli and Arthur Conan Doyle (who was promoting spiritualism).

Novelist and Daily Express correspondent Arthur Weigall amplified the idea in his essay, The Malevolence of Ancient Egyptian Spirits, by recounting personal "creepy stories."

Author Rider Haggard suggested reburning Tutankhamun to prevent ill effects on tourists.

The Power of Rumour:

"Spooky stories" spread centrally through rumour, which gained authority even from its own refutation.

Two curses were reported in the tomb: one on a clay tablet in the anti-chamber ("Death shall come on swift wings...") and another on the entrance ("Death to those who entre").

29 deaths were eventually linked to the tomb, including American robber-baron George Jay Gould, Carnarvon's half-brother Aubrey Herbert, and Governor-General Sir Lee Stack.

When curse-denier Arthur Weigall died in 1934, newspapers ran contradictory headlines: 'Arthur Weigall, who denied Tutankhamen's Cruse, is Dead' followed by 'A Curse Killed Aurthur Weigall'.

The "Unlucky Mummy" (Thomas Douglas Murry):

The case of the mummy Murry donated to the British Museum was reported in the press as early as 1904 (by Bertram Fletcher Robinson, who later died young).

This mummy was supposedly purchased by Murry in 1864; he was said to have suffered personal misfortunes (lost arm, loss of porters/party members) and others, including Mrs Warwick Hunt (to whom it was given), also suffered.

The mummy was linked to the sinking of the Titanic in 1912. Spiritualist editor W.T. Stead, who perished on the ship, had reportedly discussed the case, leading reporters to assume the casket was onboard, causing the disaster.
